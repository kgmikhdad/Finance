### 1. Corporate Finance

**Capital Budgeting:**
- **Project Evaluation:** Analyzing potential investment projects and deciding which ones to undertake based on their expected cash flows and profitability.
- **Net Present Value (NPV):** Calculating the present value of cash inflows and outflows of a project to determine its profitability. A positive NPV indicates that the project is expected to generate value.
- **Internal Rate of Return (IRR):** The discount rate that makes the NPV of a project zero. It represents the expected annual rate of return. Projects with an IRR higher than the company's required rate of return are typically accepted.

**Capital Structure:**
- **Debt vs. Equity Financing:** Deciding the mix of debt (loans and bonds) and equity (stock issuance) to finance the company's operations and growth. 
- **Optimal Capital Structure:** Finding the right balance between debt and equity to minimize the company’s cost of capital and maximize shareholder value.
- **Leverage:** Using borrowed capital to increase the potential return on investment. Higher leverage can lead to higher returns but also increases financial risk.

**Dividend Policy:**
- **Dividend Payout Ratio:** The proportion of earnings paid out as dividends to shareholders. Companies need to balance paying dividends and retaining earnings for growth.
- **Residual Dividend Model:** Dividends are paid out from the residual or leftover equity only after all project capital needs are met.
- **Stock Repurchase:** A company buys back its own shares from the marketplace, reducing the number of outstanding shares, often to increase shareholder value.

**Mergers and Acquisitions:**
- **Valuation:** Determining the value of a target company using methods such as discounted cash flow (DCF), comparables analysis, and precedent transactions.
- **Synergy:** The potential additional value generated from combining two companies, such as cost savings, increased revenues, or enhanced market reach.
- **Due Diligence:** A thorough investigation of the target company's financial, legal, and operational aspects before finalizing the merger or acquisition.
- **Post-Merger Integration:** The process of combining and reorganizing the merging companies' operations, cultures, and systems to realize the expected synergies.

**Corporate Governance:**
- **Board of Directors:** A group of individuals elected to represent shareholders and oversee the company's management and strategic direction.
- **Shareholder Rights:** Policies and practices ensuring that shareholders have a say in important company decisions, including voting on major issues.
- **Executive Compensation:** Structuring pay and incentives for top executives to align their interests with those of the shareholders and motivate performance.

**Working Capital Management:**
- **Inventory Management:** Ensuring that the company maintains optimal inventory levels to meet demand without incurring unnecessary costs.
- **Accounts Receivable/Payable:** Managing the timing of cash inflows from customers (accounts receivable) and cash outflows to suppliers (accounts payable) to maintain liquidity.
- **Cash Management:** Efficiently managing the company's cash flow to ensure sufficient liquidity for operations while maximizing returns on idle cash.


### 2. Investment

**Portfolio Management:**
- **Asset Allocation:** The process of dividing an investment portfolio among different asset categories, such as stocks, bonds, and cash, to balance risk and reward according to an investor’s goals, risk tolerance, and investment horizon.
- **Diversification:** A risk management strategy that mixes a wide variety of investments within a portfolio. The rationale is that a diversified portfolio will, on average, yield higher returns and pose a lower risk than any individual investment found within the portfolio.
- **Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT):** A framework for constructing a portfolio of assets such that the expected return is maximized for a given level of risk. Developed by Harry Markowitz, it emphasizes the importance of diversification and the relationship between risk and return.

**Securities Analysis:**
- **Fundamental Analysis:** Analyzing a company’s financial statements, health, management, competitive advantages, and market competition to determine its intrinsic value. Key metrics include earnings, revenue, assets, liabilities, and growth prospects.
- **Technical Analysis:** Evaluating securities by analyzing statistics generated by market activity, such as past prices and volume. It uses charts and other tools to identify patterns that can suggest future activity.
- **Valuation Models:** Various methods used to estimate the value of an asset, including discounted cash flow (DCF) analysis, price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio, and price-to-book (P/B) ratio.

**Fixed Income:**
- **Bond Pricing:** Determining the fair value of a bond based on the present value of its future cash flows, which include periodic coupon payments and the repayment of principal at maturity.
- **Yield Curves:** A graphical representation of interest rates on debt for a range of maturities. It shows the relationship between the yield (interest rate) and the time to maturity for bonds of similar credit quality.
- **Duration and Convexity:** Measures of a bond’s sensitivity to interest rate changes. Duration estimates how much the price of a bond will change with a change in interest rates, while convexity accounts for the curvature in the relationship between bond prices and yields.

**Equities:**
- **Stock Valuation:** Determining the intrinsic value of a stock, often using models such as the discounted cash flow (DCF) model, dividend discount model (DDM), or relative valuation techniques like P/E ratios.
- **Dividend Discount Model:** A method of valuing a company’s stock price based on the theory that its stock is worth the sum of all of its future dividend payments, discounted back to their present value.
- **Earnings per Share (EPS):** A company’s profit divided by the outstanding shares of its common stock, serving as an indicator of a company’s profitability.

**Derivatives:**
- **Options:** Financial instruments that give the holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an asset at a specified price within a specific time period.
- **Futures:** Standardized contracts to buy or sell a specific asset at a predetermined price at a specified time in the future.
- **Swaps:** Financial agreements to exchange cash flows or other financial instruments between parties, often used to manage interest rate risk or currency risk.
- **Hedging Strategies:** Techniques used to reduce or eliminate financial risk, often through derivatives such as options and futures to offset potential losses in other investments.

**Alternative Investments:**
- **Private Equity:** Investing directly in private companies or buying out public companies to delist them from stock exchanges, focusing on long-term gains through restructuring or improving management.
- **Hedge Funds:** Pooled investment funds that employ various strategies to earn active returns for their investors, including leveraging, derivatives, and short selling.
- **Real Assets:** Physical assets such as real estate, commodities, and infrastructure, which provide diversification benefits and act as a hedge against inflation.


### 3. Financial Markets and Institutions

**Money Markets:**
- **Treasury Bills:** Short-term debt instruments issued by the government with maturities ranging from a few days to one year. They are considered safe and liquid investments.
- **Commercial Paper:** Unsecured, short-term debt instruments issued by corporations to meet their immediate funding needs. Maturities typically range from a few days to 270 days.
- **Certificates of Deposit (CDs):** Time deposits offered by banks with a specific fixed term and interest rate. CDs are insured up to a certain limit by the government, making them low-risk investments.

**Capital Markets:**
- **Stock Exchanges:** Organized and regulated financial markets where securities (stocks, bonds) are bought and sold. Major stock exchanges include the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) and NASDAQ.
- **Bond Markets:** Markets where participants can issue new debt or buy and sell existing debt securities, primarily government and corporate bonds. Bond markets play a critical role in providing long-term funding.
- **Initial Public Offerings (IPOs):** The process by which a private company offers its shares to the public for the first time, raising capital and becoming publicly traded.

**Financial Intermediaries:**
- **Banks:** Financial institutions that accept deposits from the public, offer credit, and provide other financial services like loans and mortgages. They play a crucial role in money creation and credit supply.
- **Insurance Companies:** Firms that provide risk management by pooling and redistributing risk through insurance policies. They offer life, health, property, and casualty insurance.
- **Pension Funds:** Investment funds that collect and invest retirement savings to provide pension benefits to members. They are major institutional investors in financial markets.
- **Mutual Funds:** Investment vehicles that pool funds from many investors to invest in a diversified portfolio of stocks, bonds, and other securities. They provide individual investors with professional management and diversification.

**Market Regulation:**
- **Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC):** A government agency responsible for enforcing federal securities laws and regulating the securities industry, stock and options exchanges, and other related activities to protect investors and ensure fair markets.
- **Financial Stability:** Policies and measures to ensure the stability of the financial system, prevent systemic risk, and manage financial crises. Central banks and regulatory bodies play key roles in maintaining financial stability.
- **Compliance:** Ensuring that financial institutions and market participants adhere to laws, regulations, and ethical standards to maintain market integrity and protect investors. Compliance involves monitoring, reporting, and enforcing regulatory requirements.



### 4. Personal Finance

**Budgeting:**
- **Income and Expense Tracking:** Monitoring and recording all sources of income and all expenses to understand financial inflows and outflows. Tools like spreadsheets or budgeting apps can be used for this purpose.
- **Financial Planning:** Setting short-term and long-term financial goals and creating a plan to achieve them. This includes creating an emergency fund, saving for major expenses, and planning for retirement.
- **Saving Strategies:** Implementing methods to save money, such as automating savings, reducing unnecessary expenses, and using budgeting tools to keep track of savings goals.

**Retirement Planning:**
- **401(k):** An employer-sponsored retirement savings plan that allows employees to save and invest for retirement on a tax-deferred basis. Employers may also offer matching contributions.
- **IRA (Individual Retirement Account):** A retirement savings account that provides tax advantages. Traditional IRAs offer tax-deferred growth, while Roth IRAs offer tax-free growth.
- **Pension Plans:** Retirement plans that provide a fixed monthly benefit upon retirement, typically based on salary and years of service. They can be defined benefit or defined contribution plans.
- **Annuities:** Financial products that provide a stream of income, typically for life, in exchange for an initial lump-sum payment or series of payments. They can be used to ensure a steady income in retirement.

**Insurance:**
- **Life Insurance:** Provides a death benefit to beneficiaries in the event of the policyholder's death. It can help cover funeral costs, debts, and provide financial security for dependents.
- **Health Insurance:** Covers medical expenses, including doctor visits, hospital stays, surgeries, and prescription drugs. It can be obtained through employers, government programs, or private insurers.
- **Property Insurance:** Protects against damage to property, such as homes and vehicles, due to events like fire, theft, or natural disasters. Common types include homeowners and auto insurance.

**Investing:**
- **Mutual Funds:** Pooled investment funds managed by professional portfolio managers, investing in a diversified portfolio of stocks, bonds, or other securities. They offer diversification and professional management.
- **ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds):** Investment funds that trade on stock exchanges like individual stocks. They typically track an index, sector, commodity, or other asset and offer diversification with lower fees than mutual funds.
- **Individual Stocks and Bonds:** Direct investments in individual companies (stocks) or debt securities (bonds). Stocks offer potential for capital appreciation and dividends, while bonds provide fixed income and lower risk.

**Credit Management:**
- **Credit Scoring:** A numerical representation of a person's creditworthiness based on their credit history. Key factors include payment history, credit utilization, length of credit history, and types of credit.
- **Debt Repayment:** Strategies to pay off debt, such as the debt snowball method (paying off smallest debts first) or debt avalanche method (paying off highest-interest debts first). Consolidating debts or refinancing can also help manage debt.
- **Credit Cards:** A form of revolving credit that allows for purchases up to a certain limit. Responsible use includes paying off the balance in full each month to avoid interest charges and maintaining a good credit score.


### 5. Public Finance

**Taxation:**
- **Income Tax:** A tax levied on individuals and businesses based on their income or profits. It is usually progressive, meaning higher income earners pay a higher percentage of their income in tax.
- **Corporate Tax:** A tax on the profits of corporations. It affects business decisions, investment strategies, and overall economic growth.
- **VAT/GST (Value-Added Tax/Goods and Services Tax):** A consumption tax levied on the value added to goods and services at each stage of production or distribution. It is ultimately borne by the end consumer.
- **Tax Policy:** The principles and laws that govern taxation, including tax rates, exemptions, deductions, and credits. It aims to balance revenue generation, economic efficiency, and fairness.

**Government Expenditure:**
- **Budgeting:** The process by which governments plan and allocate their financial resources. It includes estimating revenues and setting spending priorities for public services and projects.
- **Public Goods:** Goods and services provided by the government that are non-excludable and non-rivalrous, such as national defense, public parks, and street lighting. These are typically funded by taxation.
- **Social Security:** Government programs that provide financial assistance to individuals during retirement, disability, or unemployment. Examples include pensions, unemployment benefits, and disability insurance.

**Public Debt:**
- **Debt Issuance:** The process by which governments borrow money by issuing bonds or other debt instruments. This is done to finance budget deficits or specific projects.
- **Debt Management:** Strategies and policies for managing a country's debt, including the terms of borrowing, repayment schedules, and refinancing options. Effective debt management aims to minimize borrowing costs and financial risk.
- **Sovereign Risk:** The risk that a government may default on its debt obligations. It can be influenced by political instability, economic conditions, and external factors such as changes in interest rates.

**Fiscal Policy:**
- **Government Spending:** Expenditures by the government on goods and services, including infrastructure, education, healthcare, and defense. It directly affects economic activity and aggregate demand.
- **Taxation:** The use of taxes to collect revenue for government spending. It can also be used to influence economic behavior and redistribute wealth.
- **Economic Stabilization:** The use of fiscal policy to smooth out economic fluctuations, combat recessions, and control inflation. This includes adjusting spending and tax policies to manage economic cycles.


### 6. International Finance

**Foreign Exchange Markets:**
- **Exchange Rates:** The value of one currency in terms of another currency. Exchange rates can be floating (determined by market forces) or fixed (pegged to another currency or basket of currencies).
- **Currency Risk:** The risk that changes in exchange rates will affect the value of investments. This risk is significant for businesses and investors involved in international transactions.
- **Hedging:** Strategies to protect against currency risk, including using financial instruments such as forward contracts, options, and swaps to lock in exchange rates or offset potential losses.

**International Trade:**
- **Trade Policies:** Regulations and agreements that govern international trade, including tariffs, quotas, and trade agreements. These policies can influence trade balances, economic growth, and diplomatic relations.
- **Balance of Payments:** A comprehensive record of a country’s economic transactions with the rest of the world, including trade in goods and services, financial transfers, and investment flows. It consists of the current account, capital account, and financial account.
- **Trade Finance:** Financial products and services that facilitate international trade, such as letters of credit, trade credit insurance, and export financing. These tools help manage the risks and complexities of cross-border transactions.

**Global Investment:**
- **Foreign Direct Investment (FDI):** Investments made by a company or individual in one country in business interests in another country. FDI typically involves significant ownership and control over the foreign business.
- **International Portfolio Investment:** Investments in foreign financial assets, such as stocks and bonds, without obtaining significant control over the businesses. This allows investors to diversify their portfolios and tap into growth opportunities in different markets.

**International Monetary Systems:**
- **IMF (International Monetary Fund):** An international organization that promotes global monetary cooperation, financial stability, international trade, high employment, and sustainable economic growth. The IMF provides financial assistance and policy advice to member countries.
- **World Bank:** An international financial institution that provides loans and grants to the governments of poorer countries for the purpose of pursuing capital projects. The World Bank aims to reduce poverty and support development.
- **Currency Regimes:** Systems by which countries manage their currencies in relation to other currencies. These include floating exchange rates, fixed exchange rates, and pegged exchange rates. Currency regimes affect international trade, investment flows, and economic stability.


### 7. Behavioral Finance

**Psychological Biases:**
- **Overconfidence:** A common bias where investors overestimate their knowledge, skills, and the accuracy of their information. This can lead to excessive trading, underestimating risks, and poor investment decisions.
- **Loss Aversion:** The tendency for people to prefer avoiding losses over acquiring equivalent gains. This can lead to holding onto losing investments too long and selling winning investments too early.
- **Herd Behavior:** The phenomenon where individuals follow the actions of a larger group, often disregarding their own analysis or information. This can lead to market bubbles and crashes as people collectively buy or sell assets.

**Market Anomalies:**
- **Calendar Effects:** Patterns in stock returns that appear to be related to the time of year or month. Examples include the January effect (higher returns in January) and the Monday effect (lower returns on Mondays).
- **Momentum:** The tendency for stocks that have performed well in the past to continue performing well in the near future, and for stocks that have performed poorly to continue performing poorly. This challenges the efficient market hypothesis.
- **Overreaction:** When investors react too strongly to news, causing stock prices to move excessively in the short term. This can create opportunities for contrarian investors who buy undervalued stocks or sell overvalued ones.

**Investor Behavior:**
- **Decision-Making Processes:** The cognitive processes by which investors make financial decisions. Behavioral finance studies how psychological factors influence these decisions, often leading to irrational outcomes.
- **Risk Perception:** How investors perceive and evaluate risk, which can differ significantly from actual risk. Psychological factors can lead to underestimation or overestimation of risks.
- **Heuristics:** Simple, efficient rules or mental shortcuts used by investors to make decisions. While heuristics can speed up decision-making, they can also lead to systematic biases and errors. Common heuristics include the availability heuristic (basing decisions on readily available information) and the representativeness heuristic (judging probabilities based on how much one event resembles another).


### 8. Quantitative Finance

**Financial Modeling:**
- **Pricing Models:** Mathematical models used to determine the fair value of financial instruments. Key models include the Black-Scholes model for options pricing, the binomial options pricing model, and discounted cash flow (DCF) analysis for valuing stocks and bonds.
- **Simulation:** Techniques such as Monte Carlo simulation, which uses random sampling to model and analyze the behavior of financial systems and assess risk and uncertainty in investment decisions.
- **Optimization:** Methods to find the best investment strategies and portfolios under given constraints. This includes mean-variance optimization for portfolio selection, which aims to maximize return for a given level of risk or minimize risk for a given level of return.

**Statistical Methods:**
- **Regression Analysis:** A statistical technique for modeling and analyzing the relationships between variables. It helps in understanding how the typical value of the dependent variable changes when any one of the independent variables is varied.
- **Time Series Analysis:** Methods for analyzing time series data to extract meaningful statistics and characteristics. It is used for forecasting future values based on previously observed values. Key techniques include ARIMA models, exponential smoothing, and GARCH models.
- **Stochastic Processes:** Mathematical frameworks to model systems that evolve over time with inherent randomness. They are used in finance to model stock prices, interest rates, and other economic variables. Examples include Brownian motion and Poisson processes.

**Computational Finance:**
- **Algorithmic Trading:** The use of computer algorithms to automate trading strategies. Algorithms can execute trades at speeds and frequencies that are impossible for human traders. Strategies can be based on a wide range of factors, including market data, news, and even social media sentiment.
- **High-Frequency Trading (HFT):** A specialized form of algorithmic trading that involves executing a large number of orders at extremely high speeds. HFT firms use sophisticated algorithms and high-speed data networks to capitalize on minute price discrepancies.
- **Machine Learning Applications:** The use of machine learning techniques to analyze and predict financial markets. This includes supervised learning for predicting stock prices, unsupervised learning for identifying trading patterns, and reinforcement learning for developing trading strategies. Machine learning can handle large datasets and uncover complex relationships that traditional statistical methods might miss.



### 9. Real Estate Finance

**Property Valuation:**
- **Appraisal Methods:** Techniques used to determine the value of a property. These include:
  - **Sales Comparison Approach:** Valuing a property by comparing it to similar properties that have recently sold in the same area.
  - **Cost Approach:** Determining the value of a property by calculating the cost to replace or reproduce the structure, minus depreciation, plus the value of the land.
  - **Income Approach:** Valuing a property based on the income it generates, often using methods like the capitalization rate (cap rate) or discounted cash flow (DCF) analysis.
- **Comparative Market Analysis (CMA):** An estimate of a property's value based on the sale prices of similar properties in the same area. It is commonly used by real estate agents to help sellers set listing prices and buyers make offers.

**Real Estate Investment:**
- **REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts):** Companies that own, operate, or finance income-producing real estate. They provide investors with a way to invest in real estate without having to buy properties directly. REITs can be publicly traded or privately held.
- **Property Development:** The process of buying land, financing real estate deals, building or renovating structures, and selling or leasing the finished product. It involves a range of activities from site selection and acquisition to construction and marketing.
- **Real Estate Syndication:** A partnership between multiple investors to pool their resources and invest in real estate projects. Syndication allows individual investors to participate in larger deals and diversify their portfolios without having to manage properties themselves.

**Mortgage Finance:**
- **Mortgage Types:** Different forms of loans used to finance the purchase of real estate. Key types include:
  - **Fixed-Rate Mortgages:** Loans with a fixed interest rate and fixed payments over the life of the loan.
  - **Adjustable-Rate Mortgages (ARMs):** Loans with interest rates that can change periodically based on market conditions, often starting with a lower initial rate.
  - **Interest-Only Mortgages:** Loans where the borrower pays only the interest for a set period before beginning to pay both principal and interest.
- **Securitization:** The process of pooling various types of debt, including mortgages, and selling the consolidated debt as bonds to investors. This allows lenders to free up capital to issue more loans.
- **Mortgage-Backed Securities (MBS):** Financial instruments backed by a pool of mortgages. Investors receive periodic payments similar to bond coupons, which are derived from the underlying mortgage payments. MBS are a key component of the real estate finance market and provide liquidity to lenders.



### 10. Risk Management

**Risk Identification:**
- **Credit Risk:** The risk of loss arising from a borrower failing to repay a loan or meet contractual obligations. It is a significant concern for banks and other lending institutions.
- **Market Risk:** The risk of losses in financial markets due to adverse price movements in assets such as stocks, bonds, commodities, and currencies. This includes interest rate risk, equity risk, and foreign exchange risk.
- **Operational Risk:** The risk of loss resulting from inadequate or failed internal processes, people, systems, or external events. This includes risks from fraud, system failures, legal issues, and other operational disruptions.

**Risk Measurement:**
- **Value at Risk (VaR):** A statistical technique used to measure the potential loss in value of a portfolio over a defined period for a given confidence interval. It provides a quantifiable measure of market risk.
- **Stress Testing:** A method of evaluating how certain stress conditions would affect a financial institution's performance. This involves simulating extreme but plausible adverse economic scenarios to assess resilience.
- **Scenario Analysis:** Analyzing the impact of different hypothetical events or changes in market conditions on a portfolio or institution. It helps in understanding the potential outcomes and preparing for adverse situations.

**Risk Mitigation:**
- **Hedging:** Using financial instruments such as derivatives (options, futures, swaps) to offset potential losses in investments. Hedging strategies are used to manage exposure to various risks, including market risk and currency risk.
- **Insurance:** Transferring risk to an insurance company in exchange for premium payments. Insurance policies can cover a wide range of risks, including property, liability, and health risks.
- **Risk Transfer:** Shifting the risk from one party to another through various means such as outsourcing, insurance, or contractual agreements. This helps in managing and distributing risks more effectively.

**Regulatory Framework:**
- **Basel III:** An international regulatory framework developed by the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision to strengthen regulation, supervision, and risk management within the banking sector. It includes requirements for capital adequacy, stress testing, and market liquidity risk.
- **Dodd-Frank Act:** A comprehensive piece of financial reform legislation enacted in the United States in response to the 2008 financial crisis. It aims to reduce systemic risk, increase transparency, and protect consumers. Key provisions include the creation of the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) and the Volcker Rule, which restricts proprietary trading by banks.
- **Solvency II:** An EU directive that codifies and harmonizes the regulation of insurance companies across Europe. It focuses on the amount of capital that insurance companies must hold to reduce the risk of insolvency and ensure they can meet their obligations to policyholders.



### 11. Financial Engineering

**Derivative Products:**
- **Structured Products:** Financial instruments designed to meet specific needs that cannot be met by standard financial instruments. These products typically involve combining multiple assets, including derivatives, to create tailored investment solutions. Examples include structured notes and equity-linked securities.
- **Exotic Options:** Options that have more complex features than standard American or European options. Examples include barrier options, Asian options, and digital options. They are often used to hedge specific risks or to take advantage of particular market conditions.
- **Collateralized Debt Obligations (CDOs):** Structured financial products backed by a pool of loans and other assets. They are divided into tranches with varying degrees of risk and return. CDOs played a significant role in the financial crisis of 2007-2008 due to their complex structure and the underlying credit risk.

**Financial Innovation:**
- **Product Design:** The creation and development of new financial products to meet the changing needs of investors, companies, and markets. This includes developing new types of securities, investment vehicles, and risk management tools.
- **Financial Technology (Fintech):** The integration of technology into offerings by financial services companies to improve their use and delivery to consumers. This includes digital payments, robo-advisors, peer-to-peer lending, and crowdfunding platforms.
- **Blockchain:** A decentralized digital ledger that records transactions across many computers in such a way that the registered transactions cannot be altered retroactively. It underpins cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin and is being explored for various applications in finance, including smart contracts and transparent transaction recording.

**Quantitative Methods:**
- **Monte Carlo Simulation:** A computational algorithm that relies on repeated random sampling to obtain numerical results. It is used in finance to model the probability of different outcomes in complex systems, such as pricing derivatives and risk assessment.
- **Numerical Methods:** Techniques used to solve quantitative problems that are difficult to solve analytically. This includes methods like finite difference methods for option pricing, root-finding algorithms, and interpolation techniques.
- **Optimization Techniques:** Mathematical methods used to find the best possible solution or decision, subject to constraints. In finance, optimization is used for portfolio selection (maximizing return for a given level of risk), risk management, and in the design of trading strategies.
